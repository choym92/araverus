[CHAPTER: The Middle East Erupts]
The big story, the one overshadowing everything else, is the massive military escalation in the Middle East. The conflict began with joint U.S. and Israeli strikes on Iran, which, according to multiple reports, resulted in the death of Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei and a large number of other high-ranking Iranian officials. The stated goal from the White House is to destroy Iran's missile and naval capabilities and prevent it from acquiring nuclear weapons. President Trump has said the operation could last "four to five weeks," though he also suggested it is ahead of schedule.

The response from Iran has been swift and severe. It has launched a wave of retaliatory drone and missile attacks across the Gulf, targeting U.S. installations and allies. These attacks have now led to the first American casualties of the conflict. An Iranian suicide drone struck a commercial port in Kuwait, killing at least six U.S. service members. In a tragic and chaotic incident, it also appears three U.S. F-15E fighter jets were mistakenly downed by Kuwaiti air defenses that were responding to the incoming Iranian attacks. Thankfully, all six aircrew members ejected safely.

The economic fallout has been immediate. Iran has effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz, a critical chokepoint for global energy. About one-fifth of the world's seaborne oil passes through there, and tanker traffic has virtually ground to a halt. Adding to the crisis, Qatar, a massive energy producer, has halted its liquefied natural gas, or LNG, production after an Iranian drone attack targeted a key facility. Saudi Arabia also suspended operations at its major Ras Tanura refinery after a drone strike. For the many expatriates who moved to cities like Dubai for safety and low taxes, the conflict has brought the war right to their doorstep, with reports of damage in the UAE and other Gulf nations.

[CHAPTER: Markets on Edge]
So, what does this all mean for the markets? In a word: turmoil. The disruption to energy supplies sent oil and gas prices soaring. Brent crude, the global benchmark, jumped over 8 percent to nearly $79.19 a barrel, while European natural gas prices surged by as much as 48 percent. This is stoking major inflation fears. One report noted that a prolonged conflict could add 0.6 to 0.7 percentage points to global inflation. The International Monetary Fund has already weighed in, stating the obvious: these developments make the global economic outlook far more uncertain.

Global stock markets are selling off, especially in Asia. South Korea's KOSPI index was hit particularly hard, plummeting 11 percent at one point, forcing a trading halt. The Hang Seng in Hong Kong lost over 2 percent. Investors are doing exactly what you would expect: they are fleeing to safety. Gold prices have surged, climbing above $5,400 an ounce. The U.S. dollar has also strengthened.

Interestingly, the bond market is behaving a little unusually. Normally in a crisis, investors pile into safe U.S. Treasury bonds, pushing yields down. But after an initial dip, yields have been rising. The thinking seems to be twofold. First, a long war means more government spending and more debt, which requires higher interest rates to attract buyers. Second, those soaring oil prices fuel inflation expectations, and bond investors demand higher yields to compensate. It is a complex reaction.

All this geopolitical chaos is giving central bankers a massive headache. Just a few days ago, the conversation was all about when the Federal Reserve might cut interest rates. Now, that calculus has changed. Minneapolis Fed President Neel Kashkari said the war could create conditions justifying an extended pause on any rate cuts. He is watching to see if the energy price shock will keep inflation elevated. This sentiment is echoed elsewhere, with a new OECD report warning of rising refinancing risk for governments as bond sales surge to a projected record of $18.3 trillion.

At a tense all-hands meeting, Altman defended the collaboration but admitted the initial communication was a mistake.
